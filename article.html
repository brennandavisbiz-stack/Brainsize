What your IQ Profile really means

BSBrainSize Research 6 min read Updated June 2026

Two people can finish the test with an identical score of 120 and think in completely different ways. The single number tells you how high you reached. Your profile tells you the shape of how you got there, and that shape is where the real insight lives.

Why one number isn't enough

A composite IQ score averages your performance across five cognitive domains: pattern recognition, logical reasoning, spatial reasoning, working memory, and processing speed. Averaging is useful, but it hides as much as it reveals. A person who is exceptional at spatial reasoning and average everywhere else can land on the exact same composite as someone who is evenly strong across the board.

They share a number. They do not share a mind. One of them solves problems by rotating and rebuilding structures in their head; the other adapts smoothly to whatever a problem demands. Those are different operating systems, and the single score erases the difference.

The shape, not the height

Your IQ Profile is built from the relationship between your five domain scores, not just how high they climb. We look at which domains rise above your own personal average and by how much, then match that signature to one of eight profiles.

Your profile is about the shape of your five domains, not how high any one of them reaches.

This is deliberate. It means a profile is meaningful at every ability level. You do not need an elite score to have a distinctive, useful cognitive signature, and someone in the 50th percentile gets just as much insight from their shape as someone in the 99th.

What the eight profiles capture

Each profile describes a characteristic way of approaching problems. A few examples:

  • The Architect pairs spatial and logical reasoning, building precise mental models and reasoning rigorously inside them.
  • The Quicksilver leads with processing speed, reaching correct answers before a problem has finished settling for everyone else.
  • The Explorer is balanced across all five, adapting to unfamiliar problems rather than forcing them into one style.

None of the eight is better than the others. They are descriptions of strengths and tendencies, not a ranking.

How to use yours

Read your profile as a map of where your reasoning is most fluent and where there is headroom. Your strongest domains are the tools you reach for naturally; your lower ones are not weaknesses to be ashamed of, but the areas where focused training tends to pay off fastest. That is exactly what the programs and games in your dashboard are built to develop.
