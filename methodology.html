Methodology & research

How BrainSize
measures.

A meaningful score takes more than counting right answers. Here's exactly how the test works, in plain language — written so you can judge it for yourself.

  1. The question bank

    Our AI continuously generates visual reasoning items — pattern matrices, sequence completion, spatial transformations. New items never go straight into scoring. They're first served unscored alongside calibrated questions so we can measure each item's real difficulty and discrimination from live response data before it counts toward anyone's result. Items that don't measure cleanly are retired.

  2. Adaptive testing (CAT)

    BrainSize uses computerized adaptive testing — the same approach behind modern graduate and licensure exams. After each answer, the engine updates its estimate of your ability and selects the next item to be maximally informative at your level. That's why the test is short: every question is doing real work, instead of being wasted on a difficulty far too easy or too hard for you.

  3. Scoring with item response theory

    Your responses are scored with an item response theory (IRT) model. It accounts for each item's measured difficulty, whether you answered correctly, and how quickly — a fast correct answer on a hard item carries the most signal. The resulting ability estimate is mapped onto the standardized IQ scale (mean 100, standard deviation 15).

  4. The five domains & your profile

    Items are tagged to five cognitive domains drawn from the hierarchical (Cattell–Horn–Carroll) model that underpins modern IQ research: pattern recognition, logical reasoning, spatial reasoning, working memory, and processing speed. The shape of your scores across these five — not just their height — determines your IQ Profile.

  5. Why we report a range

    Every measurement carries uncertainty. We report your free estimate as a range reflecting the standard error of measurement, and your full report includes the confidence interval around your point score. Any test that hands you a single precise number with no uncertainty is hiding how measurement actually works.

In plain terms

What BrainSize is — and isn't

What it is: a rigorous, adaptive estimate of general cognitive ability, built on the same statistical framework as serious standardized testing, delivered in about ten minutes and validated against the standardized score distribution on an ongoing basis.
What it isn't: a clinical IQ test administered by a licensed psychologist. It doesn't diagnose anything, and full-scale clinical assessments measure more domains over several hours. BrainSize is for insight and training, not medical, legal, educational, or employment decisions.

As our normative sample grows, we publish updated reliability and validity figures here. We'd rather show our work than ask you to take our word for it.
